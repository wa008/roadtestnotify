# Northern Ontario vs GTA DriveTest Pass Rates

If you live in the GTA and you've seen the pass rates at your local DriveTest centre, you might be wondering whether driving a few hours north would actually improve your chances. The short answer: the numbers say yes, and the gap is larger than most people expect.

## The Numbers Side by Side

Ontario has 12 DriveTest centres classified as Northern Ontario and 11 in the GTA. When you average the pass rates across each region, the difference is stark.

For the G2 road test, Northern Ontario centres average 78%. GTA centres average 64%. That is a 14 percentage point gap.

For the G road test, the picture is almost identical: 77% in the north versus 63% in the GTA. Same 14-point spread.

A 78% pass rate means roughly four out of five test-takers walk out with a licence. At 64%, roughly one in three fails. If you are booking in Brampton (58% G2) instead of Kapuskasing (88% G2), the statistical difference is 30 percentage points — the widest gap between any two centres in the province.

We have previously [ranked every centre individually](/blog/highest-pass-rate-drivetest-centres-ontario/), but looking at regional averages tells a different story. The strongest Northern centres — Kapuskasing (88% G2), Espanola (86%), Kenora (84%), North Bay (83%), and Timmins (83%) — post numbers that no GTA location comes close to matching. The highest G2 rate in the GTA belongs to Oakville at 70%, which still falls 8 points below the Northern Ontario average.

## What Drives a 14-Point Gap

The pass rate difference between regions is not about examiner strictness or grading standards. DriveTest uses a standardized evaluation across all centres. The gap comes from the driving environment and the test-taking population.

GTA centres sit in some of the densest traffic corridors in Canada. Test routes in Brampton, Mississauga, and the four Toronto centres pass through multi-lane intersections, heavy merge zones, and roads shared with commercial vehicles. A minor hesitation at a busy intersection can cascade into a critical error. Northern centres typically have routes on quieter roads with lower traffic volume. Fewer variables means fewer opportunities for unexpected situations during the test.

Test volume also plays a role. GTA centres process far more road tests per day than their Northern counterparts. Higher volume means tighter scheduling, less flexible timing, and a test-taking population under more time pressure. Smaller Northern centres run fewer tests, which often creates a calmer testing environment for both the examiner and the driver.

Demographics shift the aggregate numbers too. The GTA is the primary destination for new residents to Canada, many of whom are taking their first Ontario road test after learning to drive in a different country with different road rules and conventions. This does not reflect on any individual driver's ability, but it moves the regional statistics. Northern Ontario's test-taker pool skews toward locally trained drivers who have often practised extensively on the same roads the test uses.

## Northern Ontario Is Not One Number

The 78% regional average hides real variation within the north. Not every Northern centre sits in the 80s.

Thunder Bay, Northern Ontario's largest city, posts a 64% G2 pass rate — identical to the GTA average. Its G rate of 67% is only marginally higher than the GTA's 63%. Thunder Bay has the traffic patterns, intersection complexity, and test volume you would expect from a city of over 100,000 people. It behaves statistically like a GTA centre despite being 1,400 kilometres from Toronto.

On the other end, Kapuskasing — population under 8,000 — leads the entire province at 88% G2. Kenora, near the Manitoba border, hits 84%. These are small towns where the test route covers a meaningful portion of the local road network.

The pattern is consistent: within Northern Ontario, the smaller the community, the higher the pass rate tends to be. The three centres below 75% G2 in the north are Thunder Bay (64%), Dryden (72%), and New Liskeard (72%) — all relatively larger population centres compared to their neighbours. If you are considering a trip north to improve your odds, the specific destination matters as much as the direction.

## Should You Drive North for Your Test?

The data makes a compelling statistical case, but the decision is not purely about numbers.

Driving four or more hours to reach a Northern centre means taking the test on completely unfamiliar roads. You will not know the common test routes, the tricky intersections, or the local traffic patterns that regular students in that town have practised on for months. The pass rate advantage can shrink quickly if you are navigating a new town while already nervous. A well-prepared driver at a 64% centre often has better real-world odds than an anxious, disoriented driver at an 88% centre.

There are practical barriers too. You need a vehicle at the test location. If you are bringing your own car, that is an eight-hour round trip minimum for most GTA residents heading to a high-rate Northern centre. If you rely on a driving instructor's car, you will need to arrange local instruction at the destination — and not every small town has driving schools with availability for out-of-town students.

The strongest case for driving north is when you have already failed once at your local centre and the retest wait is long. If your next available slot in Brampton is three months out and Huntsville has an opening in two weeks with an 82% G2 pass rate, the math starts to favour the drive — especially if you can spend a day practising in the area beforehand.
