If you live in the GTA and have looked at Ontario's DriveTest pass rates, you have probably noticed that every high-ranking centre is somewhere far north. That is not a coincidence. The gap between Northern Ontario and the GTA is consistent, wide, and driven by factors that have nothing to do with examiner strictness.

We pulled the pass rates for all 56 Ontario DriveTest centres from the [pass rates page](/pass-rates/) and grouped them by region. Here is what the numbers show.

## The Regional Averages

Northern Ontario has 12 DriveTest centres. Their G2 pass rates range from 64% (Thunder Bay) to 88% (Kapuskasing). Averaged across all 12 locations, the Northern Ontario G2 pass rate sits at 78%.

The GTA has 11 centres. Their G2 pass rates range from 58% (Brampton) to 70% (Oakville). The GTA average sits at 64%.

That is a 14-percentage-point gap on the G2 test. For the G test, the gap is nearly identical: Northern Ontario averages 77%, while the GTA averages 63%.

## Thunder Bay: The Northern Outlier

Thunder Bay posts a 64% G2 pass rate, which would place it near the bottom half of GTA centres. Its G rate of 67% is only slightly better.

With a population over 100,000, Thunder Bay is the largest city in Northern Ontario. That size brings traffic conditions closer to what you would find in a mid-size GTA suburb: multi-lane roads, busier intersections, and heavier pedestrian activity. The test routes there are not the quiet two-lane residential streets you encounter in Kapuskasing or Espanola.

Thunder Bay is a reminder that the regional advantage is not about latitude. It is about traffic density and road complexity on the test routes. Small Northern towns have calmer driving environments, and that translates directly into higher pass rates. The same pattern holds across the province — centres in smaller communities, whether in Northern Ontario, Central Ontario, or Eastern Ontario, tend to outperform urban ones.

## Why the Gap Is 14 Points Wide

The gap is too consistent to have a single cause. Three structural differences drive it.

**Traffic complexity on test routes.** A G2 road test in Brampton involves multi-lane intersections, aggressive surrounding drivers, heavy pedestrian traffic, and school zones packed with parked cars. A G2 test in Kenora runs through quiet residential streets with clear sightlines and minimal traffic. Both tests use the same provincial scoring criteria. But the Brampton environment creates more error opportunities per minute behind the wheel. A momentary lapse in a complex intersection costs points. That same moment in a calm small-town setting often passes without incident.

**Test volume and scheduling pace.** GTA centres run far more tests per day. Kapuskasing processes a handful of road tests on a given morning. Brampton runs back-to-back appointments from open to close. High volume means tighter scheduling, less buffer time between tests, and a busier atmosphere around the test centre. Lower volume does not mean lower standards — examiners follow the same provincial checklist everywhere. But calmer conditions tend to produce calmer driving, and calmer driving produces fewer errors.

**Route predictability.** In a town of 8,000 people, there are only so many roads the test can use. Locals and driving instructors know every possible route, every stop sign, every tricky left turn. In a city like Mississauga, the test could go in many directions, and preparing for all of them requires more practice time. Knowing the roads you are being tested on removes a layer of uncertainty that otherwise causes hesitation and mistakes.

## Should You Drive North for Your Road Test

The data clearly favours Northern centres. Does that mean you should drive five hours to Kapuskasing?

For most GTA drivers, the answer is no — at least not that far north. The pass rate advantage assumes a driver who is equally prepared regardless of location. If all your practice hours have been on GTA roads, the unfamiliar environment of a Northern town can work against you. You lose the route familiarity advantage that local test-takers have, and you add the stress of a long drive and an unfamiliar area.

Travel costs add up too. A trip from Toronto to North Bay or Sudbury means gas, possibly a hotel the night before, and a full day away from work. If you fail, you need to make the same trip again.

The smarter move is often a centre that sits between the two extremes. Barrie (70% G2, 72% G), Lindsay (71% G2, 76% G), and Belleville (79% G2, 78% G) are all within a one-to-two-hour drive from the GTA and offer meaningfully higher rates than most GTA locations. These Central and Eastern Ontario centres give you much of the small-town advantage without the five-hour commitment. Check which of these centres we monitor on our [scope page](/scope/).

If you do decide to travel further, spend a few hours driving around the test centre area before your appointment. Get familiar with the intersections, speed zones, and parking areas. That preparation closes the gap between you and the local test-takers who already know those roads.

Picking a higher-pass-rate centre is half the strategy. The other half is actually getting a booking there.
