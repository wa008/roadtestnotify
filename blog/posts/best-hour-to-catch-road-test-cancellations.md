# Best Hour to Catch Ontario Road Test Cancellations

You are refreshing the DriveTest booking page hoping a slot opens up, but you have no idea when to check. Most people refresh randomly throughout the day and miss the windows that actually matter. Our monitoring data shows that cancellation releases cluster heavily in specific hours, and knowing when gives you a real edge.

We tracked every road test cancellation release across all Ontario DriveTest centres and broke the data down by hour of day. The pattern is clear: certain hours release far more slots than others.

## The Peak Window: 6 PM to 8 PM

The single best hour to catch a cancellation is 7 PM, with 185 recorded releases. The 6 PM hour follows closely at 174. Even 8 PM still produces 120 releases. This three-hour evening block accounts for 479 of the 1,539 daytime releases we tracked, roughly 31% of all cancellations concentrated into just three hours.

Why evenings? People tend to manage their schedules after work or school. Someone who decides during the day that they need to cancel often does not log in to the DriveTest system until they are home for the evening. The result is a predictable surge of newly available slots between 6 PM and 8 PM.

If you can only check the booking system once per day, check at 7 PM.

## Secondary Windows: Morning, Noon, and Afternoon

Beyond the evening peak, three other windows produce meaningful cancellation volume.

The early morning hours show a notable spike. The 6 AM hour records 107 releases, and 7 AM jumps to 113. This burst likely comes from overnight batch processing or people cancelling their appointments first thing before starting their day. Competition for slots may be lower at 6 AM simply because fewer people are actively refreshing at that hour.

The noon hour produces 113 releases, matching 7 AM exactly. This lunch-hour bump makes sense: people check their calendars over lunch, realize a conflict, and cancel. The 1 PM hour drops back to 62, so the window is narrow.

The afternoon cluster runs from 2 PM to 5 PM and is almost as productive as the evening block. The 2 PM hour stands out at 131 releases, the highest outside the evening window. The 4 PM hour produces 111 releases, and 5 PM hits 123. The 3 PM hour is quieter at 79 but still active. The combined afternoon block produces 444 releases, making it nearly as strong as the evening peak. This cluster likely reflects mid-workday schedule changes and people acting on cancellation decisions made earlier in the day.

## The Dead Zones: When Not to Bother

Between 9 AM and 11 AM, releases drop significantly. The 9 AM hour records just 32 releases, the lowest of any active hour. The 10 AM and 11 AM hours recover slightly to 66 and 63 respectively, but these are still well below the peaks. If you are refreshing during mid-morning, you are spending effort during one of the least productive windows.

After 8 PM, activity falls off a cliff. The 9 PM hour shows only 4 releases. From 10 PM through 5 AM, the system is essentially silent with zero or one release per hour. Refreshing during these overnight hours produces nothing.

The dead zones make a useful point by contrast: the difference between the worst hour (9 AM at 32 releases) and the best hour (7 PM at 185 releases) is nearly six to one. Timing your checks correctly is not a marginal improvement. It is the difference between finding slots regularly and almost never seeing one.

## A Practical Checking Schedule

Based on the data, here is an efficient schedule that covers the highest-release windows without requiring you to refresh all day:

**6:30 AM** — Catch the early morning burst. Fewer people are checking at this hour, so you face less competition for any slots that appear.

**12:00 PM** — Hit the lunch-hour bump. Quick check, takes thirty seconds.

**2:00 PM** — The afternoon cluster begins. The 2 PM hour has the third-highest release count of any hour at 131.

**6:00 PM to 7:30 PM** — This is your prime window. If you are going to refresh repeatedly during any part of the day, this is the time. The two highest-release hours run back to back here.

Four checks per day, timed to these windows, will put you in front of far more cancellation releases than random refreshing throughout the day. You can see the full hourly breakdown visualized on our [cancellation statistics page](/statistics/).
