# Best Time of Day to Catch Road Test Cancellations

You are refreshing the DriveTest booking page at 10 AM, hoping a cancellation drops. You have been at it for twenty minutes. The odds are not in your favour. Cancellation releases follow a clear daily pattern, and mid-morning is one of the quietest windows. If you want to catch an opening, you need to know when slots actually appear.

We tracked cancellation releases across all Ontario DriveTest centres and logged the hour each slot was freed up. The data covers over 1,500 individual releases. Here is what the numbers show, and you can see the full hourly chart on our [cancellation statistics page](/statistics/).

## The Evening Peak: 6 PM to 8 PM

The single busiest hour for cancellation releases is 7 PM, with 182 slots logged. The 6 PM hour comes in second at 172. By 8 PM the count drops to 120, still well above the daytime average.

This three-hour evening window accounts for 474 of the total releases we tracked, roughly 31% of all cancellations concentrated in just three hours. If you can only check the DriveTest site once a day, do it between 6 PM and 8 PM.

Why evenings? People tend to cancel appointments after their workday ends. They have had all day to decide they are not ready, their schedule changed, or they found a different date. The cancellations pile up in the early evening as people log on after dinner and adjust their bookings. This is also when people who snagged a date in the morning decide to swap it for a better one, releasing their original slot back into the system.

## The Afternoon Build: 2 PM to 5 PM

Starting at 2 PM, release volume picks up noticeably. The 2 PM hour sees 130 releases, the third-highest single hour of the day. From there, the counts run 77 at 3 PM, 110 at 4 PM, and 120 at 5 PM before the evening surge begins.

The afternoon window from 2 PM to 5 PM produces 437 total releases, nearly matching the evening peak in raw volume but spread over four hours instead of three. The pattern suggests a steady stream of cancellations through the afternoon that accelerates into the evening. If you have flexibility during work hours, the 2 PM hour is a particularly strong time to check. The dip at 3 PM is noticeable but brief, and by 4 PM the numbers climb back above 100.

Combined, the afternoon and evening hours from 2 PM through 8 PM account for the clear majority of the day's cancellation activity. This is the window where your time spent refreshing is most likely to pay off.

## The Early Morning Window: 6 AM to 7 AM

There is a smaller but meaningful spike right at the start of the day. The 6 AM hour logs 103 releases and 7 AM logs 112. This early-morning bump likely reflects overnight cancellations being processed and people making changes before their workday starts.

By 8 AM the count drops to 60, and at 9 AM it hits the day's low point at just 32 releases. So if you are an early riser, checking between 6 AM and 7 AM gives you a window where new slots appear but fewer people are competing for them. The trade-off is that the absolute volume is lower than the evening peak. You will see slots, but not as many.

There is a practical advantage to the morning window that the raw numbers do not capture. Fewer people are awake and actively refreshing at 6 AM compared to 7 PM. A slot that appears at 6:15 AM may sit unclaimed for several minutes. The same slot appearing at 7:15 PM is competing with the highest volume of both cancellations and searchers. If speed is a concern, the early morning offers less competition even with fewer total slots.

## When to Stop Refreshing

The quietest stretch runs from 9 AM to 11 AM. The 9 AM hour produces only 32 releases, the lowest of any active hour. At 10 AM and 11 AM the numbers sit at 60 and 62 respectively. If you have been refreshing the booking page all morning with no luck, the data explains why. You are searching during the day's dead zone.

There is a noon bump at 112 releases, matching the 7 AM level, likely driven by lunch-break cancellations. But it fades fast. The 1 PM hour drops back to 59.

After 8 PM, releases taper off sharply. The 9 PM hour logs only 4 releases. From 10 PM to 5 AM, the count is effectively zero. Staying up late to hunt for cancellations is not a productive strategy. The system goes quiet overnight and does not pick up again until 6 AM.

## Putting the Pattern to Work

Knowing the pattern is useful only if you act on it. Here is how to make the most of it.

The most effective manual approach is to focus your checking on two windows: the early morning from 6 AM to 7 AM and the evening from 6 PM to 8 PM. These two periods combined account for nearly half of all daily cancellation releases. The early morning has less competition from other searchers, while the evening has the highest raw volume.

If those times do not work for your schedule, the 2 PM hour is the next best single slot to check. Avoid the 9 AM to 11 AM stretch entirely. You are least likely to find anything during those hours, and your time is better spent elsewhere.

Cancellations can appear at any hour during the active day. A single slot opening at 3 PM is just as bookable as one at 7 PM. The data just tells you where to concentrate your effort if you cannot watch the site all day.
